\begin{abstract}
Vision-based tactile sensors (VBTS) such as GelSight and DIGIT let robots perceive
contact through the deformation of a soft, marker-printed gel. Simulating them
faithfully requires solving frictional elastic contact, which is expensive,
non-differentiable, and ill-suited to closed-loop control. We learn a
\emph{Fourier neural operator} (FNO) that maps a contact field to the
surface marker-displacement field, and use it as a differentiable,
FEM-fidelity, hardware-light surrogate for the contact solver. Trained on and
benchmarked against real finite-element (FEM) physics (PhysX deformable bodies in
Isaac Sim), the surrogate yields three results. (i)~\emph{Field-to-field framing
is the key}: because surface displacement is a \emph{non-local} (Green's-function)
response, an operator that learns global spectral convolutions beats a local
per-point MLP by $2.24\times$ and the strongest analytic and learned VBTS baselines
by $2.05$--$3.51\times$ on real physics. (ii)~\emph{Differentiability buys
sample-efficient control}: learning a goal-conditioned policy by backpropagation
through the frozen operator needs $\sim$$64\times$ fewer model queries and runs
$24\times$ faster than a gradient-free baseline at equal final accuracy.
(iii)~\emph{The sensor is differentiable end-to-end}: a torch marker-dot renderer
composes with the operator so that the applied shear can be recovered from the
rendered tactile image alone. We close with an honest account of the method's
limits, including an irreducible tangential accuracy ceiling and a speed advantage
that is durable in latency and differentiability rather than raw throughput.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Vision-based tactile sensors (VBTS)---GelSight~\cite{yuan2017gelsight},
DIGIT~\cite{lambeta2020digit}, and marker-gel variants---give robots a dense,
high-resolution sense of touch by imaging the deformation of a soft elastomer
printed with a grid of markers. They have become a workhorse of contact-rich
manipulation, but \emph{simulating} them is hard: the underlying signal is the
solution of a frictional, hyper-elastic contact problem. High-fidelity finite
element (FEM) solvers reproduce this physics but are slow, are not differentiable,
and cannot be placed inside a control or reinforcement-learning loop. Existing
tactile simulators trade this fidelity for speed through kinematic
heuristics~\cite{wang2022tacto} or linear superposition~\cite{si2022taxim}, which
break down exactly in the stick--slip regime that manipulation cares about.

We take a different route. Rather than approximate the renderer, we learn the
\emph{operator} that the FEM solver computes: a map from a compact description of
the contact (indentation profile and tangential drive) to the full surface
marker-displacement field. We instantiate it as a Fourier neural operator
(FNO)~\cite{li2021fno} and treat it as a drop-in surrogate for the solver that is
(a)~\emph{FEM-fidelity}, validated frame-by-frame against PhysX deformable-body
ground truth; (b)~\emph{differentiable}, so gradients flow through it by autograd;
and (c)~\emph{hardware-light and mesh-free}, running on a single consumer GPU.

This positions our work squarely at the fusion of embodied AI and soft robotics:
a fast, differentiable model of a soft tactile sensor that can sit inside a
control loop. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A non-locality argument for tactile operator learning.}
We show that posing the problem as \emph{field$\to$field} (not per-point
regression) is decisive: surface displacement is a non-local Green's-function
response, so a global spectral operator beats a local MLP by $2.24\times$, and
beats reimplemented TACTO-, Taxim/FOTS-, and Cattaneo--Mindlin-style baselines by
$2.05$--$3.51\times$, all measured on the \emph{same} real FEM ground truth
(Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-accuracy}).
\item \textbf{Differentiable control through a frozen operator.}
Backpropagating a goal-conditioned policy through the frozen surrogate reaches a
tactile target with $\sim$$64\times$ fewer model queries and $24\times$ less
wall-clock than evolution strategies at equal final accuracy
(Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-control}). We find the often-reported pathology of
differentiable simulators~\cite{suh2022diffsim} does \emph{not} bite here, and
explain why.
\item \textbf{An end-to-end differentiable sensor.}
A torch marker-dot renderer composes with the operator, $\mathrm{render}\circ
\mathrm{FNO}$, so a control action can be recovered from the rendered tactile
\emph{image} by gradient descent (Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-sensor}).
\item \textbf{An honest scope.} We report where the approach plateaus---an
irreducible tangential ceiling, a narrow margin over strong dense baselines, and a
speed advantage that lives in latency and differentiability, not raw throughput
(Sec.~\ref{sec:limits}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Simulating vision-based tactile sensors.}
TACTO~\cite{wang2022tacto} renders gel deformation kinematically without friction;
Taxim~\cite{si2022taxim} and FOTS~\cite{zhao2023fots} use example-based linear
superposition for fast optical and marker simulation. These are fast but model
contact only to first order. A recent line builds \emph{differentiable, physics-based}
tactile simulators---DiffTactile~\cite{si2024difftactile} and GPU contact solvers
such as TacIPC and Taccel~\cite{taccel2025}---which raise fidelity at the cost of a
full physics engine. Our surrogate is complementary: such solvers are excellent
\emph{ground-truth generators}, while the learned operator provides a
millisecond-latency, differentiable approximation of one frozen solver.

\textbf{Neural operators.}
FNO~\cite{li2021fno} and DeepONet~\cite{lu2021deeponet} learn maps between function
spaces and have become standard surrogates for PDE solution
operators~\cite{kovachki2023neuraloperator}. Attention-based operators such as the
Galerkin Transformer~\cite{cao2021galerkin} extend this family. We bring operator
learning to tactile contact and, crucially, exploit its non-locality and
differentiability rather than only its accuracy.

\textbf{Differentiable simulation for control.}
Differentiable physics~\cite{hu2020difftaichi} promises low-variance policy
gradients, but Suh~\textit{et al.}~\cite{suh2022diffsim} show that stiff or
discontinuous dynamics can make these gradients worse than zeroth-order estimates.
We test this directly through a learned contact operator and find the smoothing
induced by the spectral operator yields clean gradients---useful for control but,
we argue, also the source of its accuracy ceiling.

\section{Problem Formulation and Ground Truth}\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Three model tiers.} We deliberately separate roles
(Table is implicit): an \emph{exact-but-slow ground truth} (PhysX FEM) generates
data and is the yardstick; an \emph{analytic validator}
(Hertz~\cite{johnson1985contact} + Cattaneo--Mindlin~\cite{mindlin1949}) checks the
ground truth and provides a first-principles baseline; and the \emph{fast
differentiable surrogate} (FNO) is our contribution.

\textbf{FEM ground truth.} We simulate a $50\times50\times20$\,mm gel as a PhysX
deformable body in Isaac Sim, indented by a default sphere ($r{=}20$\,mm) and
sheared by tangential micro-steps to traverse the stick--partial-slip--full-slip
regimes. Each frame exports the surface displacement field
$\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{R}^{M\times3}$ sampled on a $32\times32$ marker grid. The
analytic validator matches the FEM contact radius to within $1.3\%$, confirming
fidelity in the normal regime. We sweep indenter radius $R$, friction $\mu$, and
gel stiffness $E$ to produce a benchmark of $2000$ training and $400$ test frames,
augmented with $400$ pure-normal frames to balance the contact-mode classes.

\textbf{Contact-mode taxonomy.} Each frame is labelled
no-contact / stick / partial-slip / full-slip by tangential-drive thresholds; we
use these labels both as a multitask target and for per-regime error reporting.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Field-to-field operator surrogate}\label{sec:method-fno}
Let $a$ be the input contact \emph{field} on the gel surface---an indentation-depth
profile stacked with a tangential-drive channel masked to the contact region---and
let $u$ be the output marker-displacement \emph{field}. We learn the solution
operator $\mathcal{G}_\theta : a \mapsto u$ as an FNO~\cite{li2021fno}, whose
spectral-convolution layers apply a global, learned kernel in the Fourier domain:
\begin{equation}
(\mathcal{K}v)(x) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\big(R_\theta \cdot \mathcal{F}v\big)(x),
\end{equation}
truncated to the lowest $k$ modes. This global support is the crux. Surface
displacement obeys an elastic Green's response: indenting one point moves a whole
neighbourhood. A \emph{local} map (a per-point MLP fed the full parameter vector)
can fit the easy param$\to$field direction but \emph{collapses} on field$\to$field
($\relL=0.74$, $63^\circ$ direction error), whereas the FNO holds. The operator
framing is therefore not a modelling convenience but the source of the result.

We add two lightweight classification heads that predict the contact mode, trained
multitask with the regression objective.

\subsection{Differentiable marker-dot sensor}\label{sec:method-sensor}
Because the operator already outputs the surface field, a tactile \emph{image}
needs no new physics: we project the deformed marker positions through a pinhole
camera placed beneath the membrane and splat each marker as a differentiable
Gaussian dot (with dark/bright polarity and saturation). The renderer is written in
torch, so $\mathrm{render}\circ\mathcal{G}_\theta$ is differentiable end-to-end and
the pixel marker-flow is a faithful image of the in-plane displacement
(Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-sensor}).

\subsection{Differentiable control through a frozen operator}\label{sec:method-control}
We freeze the trained operator (\texttt{eval}, \texttt{requires\_grad\_(False)}) so
gradients flow only to the action. Since the operator is a static one-step map, we
learn a \emph{goal-conditioned} (amortized) policy
$\pi_\phi:(\mu,E,R,\text{geom},\,\text{target summary})\mapsto(s_x,s_y)$ that drives
the contact to a desired tactile field, with loss
$\lVert\mathcal{G}_\theta(\pi_\phi(\cdot))-u^\star\rVert^2$. The same network can be
trained against an image-space reward through the renderer (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}).
We compare autograd training against evolution strategies (ES)~\cite{salimans2017es},
which treat the operator as a black box.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}

\textbf{Setup.} All models are trained and evaluated on the same swept FEM
benchmark ($2000$/$400$ train/test frames, $32\times32$ field, sphere indenter)
on a single RTX-class GPU. We report relative $L_2$ error ($\relL$), tangential
direction error, contact-mode F1, throughput, and (for control) model queries and
wall-clock. Reported control numbers are means over three seeds.

\subsection{RQ1: Accuracy and the non-locality argument}\label{sec:exp-accuracy}

Table~\ref{tab:bakeoff} reports an in-house bake-off: we reimplement the
\emph{marker-motion core} of representative VBTS simulators and fit each on our FEM
ground truth (cross-paper numbers are not comparable, since methods target
different sensor outputs). The operator attains $\relL=0.144$ and a
$14.6^\circ$ tangential direction error, beating every classical and analytic
baseline by $2.05$--$3.51\times$. Two readings stand out. First, first-principles
Cattaneo--Mindlin physics, even after affine calibration, \emph{loses} to a
data-fit linear model: affine scaling cannot change the \emph{shape} of the profile
where the real field departs from the idealised analytic one. Second, the
linear Taxim/FOTS model is the strongest classical baseline yet still trails the
operator, because the stick$\to$partial$\to$full-slip transition is
\emph{non-linear}. The operator's gain is thus non-linear \emph{and} non-local.

Against modern dense/operator architectures the margin is honest and narrow:
DeepONet and a Galerkin Transformer reach $0.210$, while a U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} matches the FNO
($0.148$ vs.\ $0.144$) with far fewer parameters. The defensible message is
\emph{``dense, non-local operator learning beats physics-based and linear tactile
models,''} not that the FNO is uniquely best. Per regime, the operator degrades
gracefully from normal ($0.112$) through full slip ($0.168$). The multitask
slip head reaches macro-F1 $0.904$ (binary slip-F1 $0.967$).

\subsection{RQ2: Generalization}\label{sec:exp-gen}
Extrapolating to the held-out upper tail of each physical parameter degrades the
operator only mildly: $1.28\times$ for radius, $1.33\times$ for friction, and
$1.27\times$ for stiffness, indicating a smooth, in-envelope response. Encoding
geometry \emph{into the input field} (rather than a one-hot tag) generalizes far
better to an out-of-distribution flat-punch indenter, since the contact field
itself carries the shape.

\subsection{RQ3: Speed}\label{sec:exp-speed}
The operator runs at $\sim$$8000$ frames/s ($\sim$$0.125$\,ms latency) versus
$0.34$\,frames/s for the single-threaded FEM shear solver---a $\sim$$23{,}000\times$
speedup (Fig.~\ref{fig:fidspeed}). We are careful here (Sec.~\ref{sec:limits}):
modern batched GPU physics engines achieve higher aggregate \emph{throughput}. The
operator's durable advantage is the \emph{combination} of low single-frame latency,
cheap differentiability, light hardware, and mesh-freedom---not raw fps.

\subsection{RQ4: Differentiable control}\label{sec:exp-control}
We train the goal-conditioned servo policy two ways
(Table~\ref{tab:control}, Fig.~\ref{fig:control}). Autograd through the frozen
operator and black-box ES reach statistically indistinguishable final loss
($8.0$ vs.\ $8.4\times10^{-8}$), but autograd uses $\mathbf{300}$ forward
evaluations against ES's $\mathbf{19{,}200}$ ($64\times$ fewer) and finishes in
$\mathbf{13}$\,s versus $\mathbf{314}$\,s ($24\times$ faster). Measured by queries
to first reach the target, the gap is $31$ vs.\ ${\sim}4100$ ($133\times$). Both
sit above a per-instance oracle ($1.6\times10^{-8}$), the expected price of a
single amortized network serving all contexts.

Notably, the gradient pathology of differentiable
simulators~\cite{suh2022diffsim} does \emph{not} appear: autograd gradients are
clean in both stick and slip regimes. The reason is subtle and recurs below---the
spectral operator \emph{smooths} the sharp stick$\to$slip transition, which is bad
for forward accuracy but gives smooth, well-behaved gradients for control.

\subsection{RQ5: The differentiable sensor and environment}\label{sec:exp-sensor}
The rendered marker flow is a faithful image of in-plane displacement: the cosine
between pixel flow and surface displacement is $0.973$, and a render$\to$track
round trip recovers marker centroids to $1.9$\,px at $160$\,px resolution
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sensor}). Composing the renderer with the operator, we recover the
applied shear by gradient descent: from the raw field, autograd inverts the action
to $2.3\%$ magnitude error; from the rendered \emph{image} alone, it recovers the
shear \emph{direction} to ${\sim}1.5^\circ$.\footnote{Aggregate
image-inversion accuracy over many frames is still being finalised; \todo{confirm
the multi-frame mean (report cites $2.1\%/1.1^\circ$) and update.}}
Finally, wrapping the frozen operator, the sensor, camera noise, and a reward into a
single differentiable environment, the Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-control} policy closes
$87\%$ of the random-to-oracle reward gap, and a finite-difference gradient check
passes (rel.\ error $3.7\%$), confirming gradients flow to the action through the
full image pipeline.

\section{Limitations and Honest Scope}\label{sec:limits}
We state the boundaries plainly, since they shape the contribution.
\textbf{(1) An irreducible tangential ceiling.} Tangential accuracy plateaus near
$\relL\,{\approx}\,0.15$ / $14^\circ$. We could not break it with more data,
larger models, finer FEM meshes, more Fourier modes, richer input
representations, or loading-history features---all negative. A path-dependence
test was null: the final state depends only on the endpoint, not the loading
trajectory, so contact memory is not the cause. The ceiling decomposes into a small
``sharp-but-smoothed'' component (the spectral operator low-passes the contact
edge, ${\sim}5\%$, partly patchable) and a larger component that appears
irreducible from macro parameters. This is the same smoothing that gave us clean
control gradients (Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-control}): a single mechanism, helpful for
control and limiting for accuracy.
\textbf{(2) Speed is not a durable raw advantage} (Sec.~\ref{sec:exp-speed}); the
advantage is latency $+$ differentiability $+$ light hardware.
\textbf{(3) The margin over strong dense baselines is narrow} (U-Net $\approx$ FNO).
\textbf{(4) The environment is single-step:} the operator is a static map, so
multi-step dynamics would require a temporal operator.
\textbf{(5) No hardware yet:} sim-to-real calibration exists only as a schema; we
have not closed the loop on a physical sensor.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented a Fourier neural-operator surrogate for vision-based tactile sensing
that is FEM-fidelity, differentiable, and hardware-light. The field-to-field framing
exploits the non-locality of elastic contact and beats classical and analytic
tactile models on real physics; its differentiability buys an order-of-magnitude
gain in control sample efficiency; and a torch renderer makes the whole sensor
differentiable end-to-end. We were explicit about where the method
plateaus. The natural next step is to mount the surrogate inside a full Isaac-Sim
robot environment and to close the sim-to-real loop on a physical marker-gel
sensor, fine-tuning the operator on real measurements so that reality corrects both
discretisation and model error at once.
